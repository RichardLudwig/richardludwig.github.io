I completed the Duolingo Italian course, and I have some thoughts...

- Understand that the grammar can be complex for such an "easy" language. I have worked with some Italian people in the past, so I was able to mitigate this issue by remembering how they would approach English grammar in speech, and then used memories of their "Italian" speech patterns to decipher the flow of the Italian language on Duolingo. If you don't know any Italians, you can watch YouTube videos of Italians speaking English (or your base language during the course). Alternatively, you can get an Italian tutor on <a target="_blank" href="https://www.italki.com/">Italki</a>.
- I understood a lot of the Italian language because I had prior knowledge of the basics of Spanish and French, which are somewhat related to Italian, as they share a common Latin base. The only issue this familiarity brings is that of spelling words correctly in the target language. 
- Specialize on the skills you want to learn. I completed the course because I wanted to learn to read Italian, and I can now read and understand the basics of news articles. While I don't understand every word I read, I can understand the gist of the articles. Duolingo's approach to language learning (e.g. translation) works well for this goal.  
- Listening and speaking skills are currently underdeveloped, but resources like <a target="_blank" href="https://www.pimsleur.com/">Pimsleur</a> (not free; check your local library for free copies or wait for a free week/month promotion) can assist with improving those domains of language learning for me in the future. 
- In bocca al lupo!
